Basil Leaf Automation - Senior Capstone Project

September 2019 - June 2020

Recent population and wage increases have forced farmers to grow more food without a proportionate increase in work force. Automation is a key factor in reducing cost and increasing efficiency. In this paper, we explore our automation solution that utilizes position manipulation and vision processing to identify, pick up, and drop a leaf into a can. Two stepper motors and a linear actuator drove the three-dimensional actuation. Leaf and can recognition were accomplished through edge detection and machine learning algorithms. Testing proved subsystem-level functionality and proof of concept of a delicate autonomous pick-and-place robot. For my senior capstone project at Santa Clara University, I, along with 6 other engineering students, spent 9 months designing and fabricating a robot that plays a small role in alleviating this labor shortage.

At Stanislaus Food Products, much of their tomato sauce packaging facilities is automated, with the exception of a seemingly simple task. The act of moving basil leaves from baskets to tomato sauce cans. This task, which is simple for humans, requires a level of precision and dexterity that isn't typically found in automated robots. This robot utilizes computer vision to locate basil leaves and tomato sauce cans and takes advantage of precise electromechanical movement to efficiently automate the placement of basil leaves.

My role on this team can be described as a Mechatronics Engineer. I spent most of my time writing code in Python to control the stepper motors used for precise movement and vacuum pump for the suction gripper. I also oversaw the integration between the Machine Learning algorithms and the hardware of the robot.

Some of my direct contributions included:

  • Designed a frame for the robot in SolidWorks
  • Rapidly prototyped of various nozzle shapes for the suction gripper using 3D printing
  • Developed a stepper motor class for easy implementation of movement on a Raspberry Pi
  • Wired limit switches, stepper motors, and a vacuum pump to control all necessary electromechanical actuation

I'm currently dedicating my time to further exploring possible Computer Vision and Machine Learning techniques to improve the recognition and classification of Basil Leaves using cameras. Some of these techniques include using OpenCV to find the centroid of the basil leaves. I also used Principal Component Analysis and utilized Sklearn to deploy Linear and Quadratic Discriminant Analysis models that correctly differentiate fresh leaves from rotten leaves with an accuracy of 73%.
